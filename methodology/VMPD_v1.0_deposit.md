## A Methodology for Diagnosing Structural Failures in Governance Regimes

## Abstract

Vulnerability Management for Policy Design (VMPD) is a methodology for diagnosing governance failures that arise when downstream regulatory frameworks address capability or output without governing the upstream conditions that produce them. It originates in the operational practice of cybersecurity vulnerability management and is translated, through analogical-abductive reasoning, into a diagnostic for policy and governance regimes. VMPD identifies the structural gap between what a governance framework assumes a system does and what the system actually does under operational conditions, characterises the upstream conditions that produce the gap, and surfaces them as objects of governance in their own right. This deposit articulates the methodology, its conceptual foundation, the diagnostic procedure across five sequential examinations, and an accompanying scoring framework analogous to the Common Vulnerability Scoring System (CVSS) used in cybersecurity practice. The methodology is intended for application by policy analysts, governance researchers, and practitioners working at the intersection of regulatory design and operational implementation.

## 1. Introduction and Problem Statement

Policy and governance regimes routinely fail not because their text is wrong but because the conditions of their production and operation are ungoverned. Ambiguity functions as protective design for drafters. Coordination architectures exclude actors who will be held accountable for outcomes the regime addresses. Pattern recognition functions essential to the regime's response architecture are assumed without being assigned to any responsible authority. Recourse mechanisms are formally available but operationally unreachable for the populations most affected. These are not drafting accidents. They are design choices, sustained across regimes and jurisdictions, whose costs fall on those least equipped to bear them.

Existing analytical methods address adjacent but distinct concerns. Regulatory impact assessment analyses anticipated effects of proposed policy on identified stakeholders. Algorithmic impact assessment analyses risks of specific automated systems against defined criteria. Gap analysis identifies where existing policy fails to address a named problem. Each of these methods takes the policy text or its operational object as the unit of analysis. None of them treats the conditions of the regime's production and operation as the proper object of governance analysis in their own right.

VMPD addresses this gap. It examines a governance regime by interrogating the conditions on which the regime depends but which it does not govern, the actors implicated by the regime but absent from its architecture, the ambiguities in its text that perform protective work for drafters at the cost of the governed, the pattern recognition functions it assumes without assigning, and the recourse mechanisms it formalises without making accessible. The methodology produces a structured characterisation of the regime's structural vulnerabilities, intended as the basis for subsequent design work, advocacy, or comparative analysis.

VMPD is grounded in operational practice. It originates in the cybersecurity discipline of vulnerability management, which has spent decades developing the diagnostic infrastructure required to treat unobservability and unknown system behaviour as governable conditions. The translation of that diagnostic infrastructure into policy analysis is the methodology's central intellectual move.

## 2. Conceptual Foundation

### 2.1 The Governance Theory Underlying VMPD

VMPD rests on three interlocking claims about what makes governance regimes legitimate and what makes them fail.

The first claim is that legitimacy is a design problem. Authority is not legitimate by virtue of procedural compliance with the formal requirements of legislative or regulatory enactment. It is legitimate when it is traceable to consent, when its separation of powers is operationally maintained rather than merely structurally declared, and when those affected by its decisions have genuine standing in the architecture that produces them. Where these conditions fail, the regime suffers a legitimacy vulnerability regardless of its formal validity.

The second claim is that legibility is a precondition for legitimate authority. A regime that cannot be understood by those it governs, that depends on conditions it cannot see, or that operates through processes whose specifications are assumed but not verified, cannot perform the function of legitimate governance. Legibility here is bidirectional: the governed must be able to understand the regime, and the regime must be able to see the conditions on which its operation depends. James C. Scott's analysis of state legibility identifies the pathological bias of modern institutions toward making systems measurable and manageable in service of state purposes; VMPD inverts the frame, asking whether the conditions on which a regime depends are legible to the regime itself, and whether the regime is legible to those it governs.

The third claim is that ambiguity in policy text is a design feature rather than a drafting accident. Ambiguity in operative provisions performs protective work for drafters and for the most powerful actors within a regime's scope. It defers accountability, enables discretionary enforcement, and shifts the cost of interpretation onto those least equipped to bear it. Flexibility in policy text to accommodate context is legitimate; ambiguity in enforcement triggers, accountability assignments, or recourse mechanisms is not. The distinction matters because conflating them allows protective ambiguity to be defended as necessary nuance.

These three claims are connected. Legitimacy depends on legibility; legibility is undermined by protective ambiguity; protective ambiguity is sustained by the structural exclusion of those it most affects. A methodology that diagnoses governance failure must examine each of these conditions and their interactions.

### 2.2 The Analogical-Abductive Move

VMPD draws its diagnostic procedure from the operational practice of cybersecurity vulnerability management. The translation is analogical and abductive: it does not claim that policy regimes are software systems or that governance failures are technical exploits. It claims that the diagnostic logic developed in cybersecurity to address unobservability, unknown system behaviour, and the gap between assumed and actual operation is structurally applicable to policy regimes facing analogous failures.

Cybersecurity vulnerability management treats "what we do not know we do not know" as a governable condition. The Common Vulnerabilities and Exposures (CVE) catalogue exists because the field learned, through repeated failure, that vulnerabilities cannot be addressed if they are not surfaced, that surfacing requires structured disclosure, and that disclosure requires a methodology for distinguishing genuine vulnerabilities from noise. The Common Vulnerability Scoring System (CVSS) exists because addressing surfaced vulnerabilities requires comparable, traceable, expert-judgment-based assessment of severity. The infrastructure of CVE, CVSS, the National Vulnerability Database, and adjacent instruments constitutes a discipline that has operationalised the treatment of unobservability as a governable condition.

Policy and governance regimes face structurally identical failures. They depend on conditions they cannot see. They produce outputs whose causal chains they cannot trace. They operate through processes whose specifications are assumed but not verified. They suffer from the gap between what they assume systems do and what systems actually do. These failures are not analogous to cybersecurity vulnerabilities in their content; they are analogous in their structure. VMPD imports the diagnostic logic developed for the cybersecurity case into the policy case, adapting it to the materials and stakes of governance analysis.

The abductive element is that VMPD does not derive from policy theory. It is derived from practitioner observation that the diagnostic moves which work in cybersecurity, when attempted in policy contexts, surface failures that policy-native methods do not see. The methodology's defence is not theoretical elegance but operational utility: applied to specific regimes, it produces findings that other methods miss, and those findings characterise structural failures whose remediation matters.

### 2.3 What VMPD Adds

The methodology contributes three things that existing policy analysis methods do not provide.

It treats the conditions of policy production and operation as the proper object of analysis. Other methods take the policy text, its anticipated effects, or its operational object as the unit of analysis. VMPD takes the production and operational conditions themselves as the unit of analysis, surfacing structural features of those conditions as governable objects in their own right.

It imports a discipline that has operationalised the treatment of unobservability. Cybersecurity vulnerability management is an unusual discipline in that it has built the institutional infrastructure required to address what is not directly observable. Most policy analysis methods, by contrast, restrict themselves to what is observable in the text or its declared operation. VMPD extends the analytical reach to what the regime depends on but does not see.

It produces structured outputs intended for subsequent design work. The methodology is diagnostic, not prescriptive. Its outputs are characterised structural vulnerabilities, each located in the regime's text or architecture, with traceable analyst rationale. These outputs are intended to support subsequent design choices by drafters, affected parties, governance designers, or comparative researchers — but VMPD itself does not prescribe specific governance instruments to remedy surfaced vulnerabilities.

## 3. The Diagnostic Procedure

VMPD analyses a governance regime — a policy text, regulatory framework, coordination architecture, or proposed instrument — by examining the conditions of its production and operation rather than the text itself. The diagnostic proceeds through five sequential examinations. Each examination produces outputs that feed the next.

### 3.1 Inputs

VMPD requires three inputs from the analyst.

The governance artifact under analysis: the policy text, regulatory framework, coordination document, or proposed instrument. VMPD can be applied to enacted regimes, drafts, consultation documents, or proposed frameworks at any stage of development.

The operational context in which the regime is intended to function: the actors involved in implementation, the systems or activities being governed, the populations affected, and the existing institutional architecture into which the regime is being introduced or within which it operates.

The accountability frame: the explicit or implicit claims the regime makes about who is responsible for what outcomes, including outcomes the regime does not directly address but which fall within its scope of concern.

### 3.2 The Five Diagnostic Examinations

#### Examination 1: Legibility Analysis

The diagnostic begins by identifying where the regime depends on conditions, processes, or system behaviours that are assumed observable or auditable but are not in fact legible to the regime's enforcement mechanisms. This includes upstream conditions that produce the outputs the regime governs, intermediate processes presumed compliant by virtue of prior use, and system behaviours whose specifications are assumed but not verified.

The output is a characterised set of conditions on which the regime depends but which it cannot see, with each condition located in the regime's text or operational architecture and characterised in terms of why the assumed legibility fails.

#### Examination 2: Architecture Analysis

The diagnostic then examines the coordination architecture the regime establishes or assumes. The examination asks: which actors are structurally required to participate in implementation, enforcement, or oversight? Which actors will be held accountable for outcomes the regime addresses, whether or not they are included in its architecture? Which actors have domain authority over conditions the regime depends on but does not formally include?

The output is a map of structural absences — actors who will bear accountability for outcomes the regime addresses, or whose authority is implicated by conditions the regime depends on, but who are not structurally required in its coordination architecture.

#### Examination 3: Specificity Analysis

The diagnostic examines the regime's enforcement provisions for ambiguity that performs protective work. The examination asks: where does the text use language that signals commitment without enforceable specificity? Where are obligations stated in ways that defer accountability to undefined future processes? Where do thresholds, triggers, or required actions depend on judgments that no authority is required to make?

The output is a characterised set of provisions where ambiguity is doing structural work, with each instance located in the regime's text and characterised in terms of who benefits from the ambiguity and who bears its costs.

#### Examination 4: Pattern Responsibility Analysis

The diagnostic examines the regime's response architecture for assumed pattern recognition functions that are not assigned. The examination asks: where does the regime assume that someone is monitoring for conditions, trends, or precursor patterns whose detection would trigger the regime's response mechanisms? Is that monitoring function assigned to a specific authority with the capability and mandate to perform it? If a pattern develops, who is responsible for naming it as the pattern the regime anticipated?

The output is a characterised set of pattern recognition assumptions that have no responsible authority — gaps where the regime expects timely response but has not assigned the function that would produce it.

#### Examination 5: Recourse Analysis

The diagnostic examines the regime from the position of those it claims to protect or whose interests it implicates. The examination asks: what mechanisms exist for affected parties to surface concerns, contest decisions, or trigger review before the regime's enforcement actions are taken? Are those mechanisms accessible without legal or institutional resources the affected parties are unlikely to have? Where the regime depends on affected parties bearing the cost of enforcement through complaints, litigation, or public pressure, is that cost structure named or hidden?

The output is a characterisation of the regime's recourse architecture and its accessibility, including identification of any populations for whom the regime's protections are formally available but operationally unreachable.

### 3.3 Outputs

The diagnostic produces a structured characterisation of the regime's structural vulnerabilities along five dimensions: invisible conditions, absent actors, protective ambiguities, unassigned pattern functions, and inaccessible recourse. Each vulnerability is named, located in the regime's text or architecture, and characterised in terms of which actors benefit from the vulnerability and which populations are impacted by it.

The output is diagnostic, not prescriptive. VMPD surfaces what is structurally wrong with the regime as designed; it does not specify which governance instruments would remedy the surfaced vulnerabilities, because that determination depends on contextual factors, political feasibility, and instrument design choices that fall outside the diagnostic itself.

### 3.4 Sequential Dependence

The five examinations are sequential because each builds on the previous. Legibility analysis precedes architecture analysis because identifying invisible conditions clarifies which actors have domain authority over those conditions and should therefore be in the architecture. Architecture analysis precedes specificity analysis because identifying absent actors clarifies whose interests are being protected by ambiguous provisions. Specificity analysis precedes pattern responsibility analysis because the regime's response mechanisms can only be examined for unassigned functions once the substantive provisions are characterised. Pattern responsibility analysis precedes recourse analysis because the recourse architecture's adequacy depends on what the regime requires of affected parties given the pattern functions it does or does not assign.

The procedure can be applied iteratively, with later examinations surfacing conditions that warrant returning to earlier examinations. In practice, a complete VMPD analysis of a substantial governance regime typically requires multiple passes.

## 4. The VMPD Scoring Framework

The diagnostic procedure produces structured findings. The scoring framework makes those findings comparable across regimes, traceable in their reasoning, and defensible through documented analyst judgment. The framework is analogous in design to the Common Vulnerability Scoring System (CVSS) used in cybersecurity practice: it does not remove expert judgment but constrains and structures it, producing scores whose value is reproducibility and comparability rather than automation.

### 4.1 The Unit of Analysis

A complete VMPD analysis identifies multiple discrete structural vulnerabilities within a regime. Each identified vulnerability receives its own scored profile. The regime's overall vulnerability picture is the aggregated set of scored vulnerabilities, not a single composite score. This parallels the CVE/CVSS structure in which a software system has many discrete CVEs, each with its own CVSS score, and the system's overall security posture is the aggregated vulnerability profile.

This unit-of-analysis choice avoids the false reduction of a complex regime to a single number and preserves the analytical specificity that makes VMPD useful for subsequent design work.

### 4.2 Scoring Dimensions

Each identified vulnerability is scored along the five examination dimensions, with sub-metrics for each. Sub-metrics are scored on a defined scale (0–4, where 0 indicates no vulnerability on this sub-metric and 4 indicates severe vulnerability), with published criteria for each level.

#### Legibility Dimension

- Upstream Conditioning Visibility: degree to which the regime requires attestation, audit, or disclosure of upstream conditions on which downstream provisions depend.
- Process Specification: degree to which critical processes are specified to a level permitting verification.
- Inherited Trust: degree to which the regime presumes compliance based on prior use rather than verification.
- Verification Authority: presence and adequacy of a named authority with mandate and capability to verify legibility claims.

#### Architecture Dimension

- Accountability-Inclusion Ratio: proportion of actors who will be held accountable for outcomes who are also structurally required in the coordination architecture.
- Domain Authority Coverage: proportion of domains the regime depends on whose authorities are formally included.
- Affected Party Standing: degree to which populations affected by the regime have formal standing in its architecture.
- Coordination Specificity: degree to which the regime specifies coordination mechanisms rather than assuming them.

#### Specificity Dimension

- Threshold Definition: degree to which triggering conditions are defined to a level permitting enforcement without further interpretation.
- Obligation Bindingness: degree to which obligations are stated as enforceable requirements rather than aspirational commitments.
- Deferred Process Density: count and weight of provisions deferring substantive determination to undefined future processes.
- Exception Architecture: degree to which exceptions are bounded and conditioned rather than open-ended.

#### Pattern Responsibility Dimension

- Pattern Recognition Assignment: presence of a named authority responsible for identifying the patterns the regime's response mechanisms anticipate.
- Detection Capability Requirement: degree to which the named authority is required to have or build the capability to perform pattern recognition.
- Trigger Specificity: degree to which the conditions that should trigger response are specified to a level permitting the named authority to act.
- Response Architecture Coherence: degree to which the response mechanisms are matched to the patterns they are meant to address.

#### Recourse Dimension

- Mechanism Existence: presence of contestation mechanisms for affected parties.
- Mechanism Accessibility: degree to which mechanisms are accessible without resources affected parties are unlikely to have.
- Cost Distribution: degree to which the cost of triggering enforcement is borne by the regime's enforcement architecture rather than by affected parties.
- Pre-Enforcement Recourse: presence of mechanisms allowing affected parties to surface concerns before enforcement actions are taken against them.

### 4.3 Score Calculation

Each dimension produces a sub-score on a 0–10 range, calculated from the analyst's sub-metric judgments using equal weighting in v1.0. The five dimension sub-scores combine into a Base Vulnerability Score on a 0–10 range. Higher scores indicate more severe structural vulnerability.

The output for each identified vulnerability is the vector of dimension sub-scores, the Base Vulnerability Score, and the analyst's documented rationale for each metric judgment. Visualisation as a radar chart of dimension sub-scores is the recommended graphical form.

### 4.4 Temporal and Contextual Modifiers

In addition to the Base Vulnerability Score, two modifier categories may be applied:

Temporal modifiers capture how the vulnerability profile changes over time as the regime is amended, as enforcement practice develops, as adjacent regimes shift, or as the operational context evolves. A regime can score worse over time as the conditions it assumes diverge from operational reality, even without textual amendment. Temporal modifiers should be applied with documented rationale tied to specific changes.

Contextual modifiers capture how the vulnerability profile shifts when the regime is applied to a specific operational context, jurisdiction, or affected population. The same regime can produce different vulnerability profiles in contexts with strong civil society infrastructure versus weak, or for populations with strong existing recourse versus none.

Modifier application is optional and context-dependent. The Base Score is the comparable cross-regime measure; modified scores are the contextually-applicable measures.

### 4.5 The Role of Expert Judgment

VMPD scoring depends entirely on analyst expertise. The scoring framework constrains and structures judgment but does not replace it. Two analysts scoring the same regime should arrive at similar scores not because the framework removes their judgment but because the published criteria and required documentation discipline the judgments they make. Where analysts disagree, the disagreement is itself analytically productive: it surfaces which sub-metric judgments are contested and why, and supports refinement of the scoring criteria over subsequent versions of the framework.

This is the same posture CVSS occupies in cybersecurity practice. The methodology's defence is not that it is objective in the sense of being judgment-free, but that it makes judgment structured, traceable, and comparable.

## 5. Worked Example: Pipeline Attestation and Digital Rights

The first published application of VMPD is Bhardwaj (2026), _Security-by-design: A Case for Digital Rights Enforcement Through Data Pipeline Attestations_. The paper applies VMPD to the General Data Protection Regulation (GDPR) and the EU AI Act, surfacing the structural vulnerability the paper terms the _Lovelace Threshold_: the gap between what current rights frameworks assume systems do (execute predefined instructions in fully legible and specifiable ways) and what generative and agentic systems actually do (anticipate, generate, and act beyond explicit specification).

The diagnostic surfaces the upstream data-conditioning layer as an ungoverned condition on which downstream rights enforcement depends. Pipeline attestation emerges from the analysis as a proposed governance instrument addressing the surfaced condition: a certification requirement analogous to pre-market conformity assessments, requiring documentation of selection criteria, transformation logic, and withdrawal mechanisms, verified by independent auditors before system deployment.

A complete VMPD analysis of the regimes examined in the paper, including dimension scores for the discrete vulnerabilities identified, is provided as the first worked example in the accompanying GitHub repository. Subsequent worked examples will be added as additional regimes are analysed.

## 6. Application Criteria, Limitations, and Scope

VMPD applies to governance regimes where downstream enforcement depends on upstream conditions, where actors will be held accountable for outcomes they have limited role in shaping, where pattern recognition is assumed without being assigned, or where recourse mechanisms are formalised without operational accessibility. The methodology is most productive when applied to regimes with substantial operational complexity, multiple implicated actors, and identifiable populations affected by the regime's operation.

VMPD requires from the analyst: the governance artifact under analysis, contextual knowledge of the operational environment in which the regime functions, and willingness to surface findings that may be politically inconvenient for the regime's drafters or supporters. The methodology produces findings that frequently identify structural choices that benefit drafters at the cost of governed populations; analysts should anticipate that such findings may be received as critique.

VMPD does not specify which governance instruments would remedy surfaced vulnerabilities. That determination depends on contextual factors, political feasibility, and instrument design choices that fall outside the diagnostic. The methodology's output is intended to support subsequent design work, not to replace it.

VMPD is not a substitute for regulatory impact assessment, algorithmic impact assessment, or other established policy analysis methods. It is complementary: where those methods analyse the policy text and its declared operation, VMPD analyses the conditions of production and operation. The methods can be applied in sequence or in parallel, with each producing distinct analytical outputs.

The scoring framework in v1.0 uses equal weighting across sub-metrics and dimensions. Subsequent versions are expected to refine weighting based on application experience and methodological critique. Analysts applying VMPD are encouraged to document any contextual weighting decisions and the rationale for them.
